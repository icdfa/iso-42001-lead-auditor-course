# ISO/IEC 42001 Lead Auditor Training Course: The Complete Guide

## Module 9: Closing the Audit and Follow-up

### Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, you will be able to:
1. Prepare comprehensive audit conclusions based on the evaluation of findings.
2. Conduct an effective, professional closing meeting with the auditee's management.
3. Write a clear, accurate, and actionable final audit report.
4. Understand the auditee's process for root cause analysis and corrective action planning.
5. Explain the auditor's role in reviewing corrective action plans, verifying implementation, and closing nonconformities.

### 9.1 Preparing Audit Conclusions: The Final Verdict
Before presenting findings to the auditee, the audit team must meet privately to review the audit findings and any other appropriate information collected during the audit against the audit objectives. The team must agree on the final audit conclusions.

**Audit conclusions address high-level issues such as:**
- The overall extent of conformity of the AIMS with ISO/IEC 42001 requirements.
- The effective implementation, maintenance, and improvement of the AIMS (is the system actually working, or is it just paperwork?).
- The capability of the management review process to ensure the continuing suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness of the AIMS.
- If the audit objectives include certification (third-party audit), the conclusion will include a formal recommendation on whether certification should be granted, maintained, suspended, or withdrawn.

### 9.2 Conducting the Closing Meeting: Presenting the Results
The closing meeting, facilitated by the Lead Auditor, is held to present the audit findings and conclusions to the auditee's management. This meeting should be formal but constructive. There should be no surprises; good auditors communicate potential nonconformities to the auditee as they are found during the audit.

**Key elements of a successful closing meeting:**
- **Gratitude:** Thank the auditee for their time, cooperation, and transparency.
- **Context:** Reiterate the audit scope, objectives, and criteria.
- **The Sampling Disclaimer:** Crucially, explain that the audit was based on a sampling process. This introduces an element of uncertainty. Emphasize that the absence of identified nonconformities does *not* guarantee that no nonconformities exist in the AIMS.
- **The Good News:** Present the audit findings, starting with positive observations, areas of strong conformity, and best practices observed.
- **The Findings:** Present any nonconformities clearly and objectively. Ensure the auditee understands the objective evidence and the specific requirement that was not met. Do not argue, but be prepared to explain the finding.
- **Opportunities for Improvement (OFIs):** Present OFIs as constructive suggestions, being careful not to dictate specific solutions.
- **Next Steps:** Discuss the timeframe for the auditee to present a corrective action plan for any nonconformities. Outline the next steps regarding report issuance and (if applicable) the certification decision process.

If there are diverging opinions regarding the audit findings or conclusions between the audit team and the auditee, the Lead Auditor should attempt to resolve them. If they cannot be resolved, all opinions must be formally recorded.

### 9.3 Writing the Audit Report: The Official Record
The Lead Auditor is responsible for the preparation and contents of the final audit report. The report must provide a complete, accurate, concise, and clear record of the audit. It is the official document that management (or the Certification Body) will use to make decisions.

**A standard audit report for ISO/IEC 42001 includes:**
- Audit objectives, scope, and criteria.
- Identification of the audit client, the auditee, and the audit team members.
- Dates and locations where the audit activities were conducted.
- A summary of the audit process, including any obstacles encountered that may decrease the reliability of the audit conclusions.
- The detailed audit findings (conformities, nonconformities, and OFIs) supported by the specific objective evidence.
- The overall audit conclusions, including a definitive statement on the effectiveness of the AIMS.
- A statement on the degree to which the audit criteria have been fulfilled.
- Any unresolved diverging opinions.

The report should be issued within the agreed timeframe. It is the property of the audit client, and the audit team must maintain strict confidentiality regarding its contents.

### 9.4 Audit Follow-up and Closing Nonconformities
An audit is technically completed when all planned audit activities have been carried out and the report is distributed. However, if nonconformities were identified, a critical follow-up process begins.

#### 9.4.1 The Auditee's Responsibility
The auditor does not fix the problem; the auditee does. The auditee must:
1. **Determine the Root Cause:** Why did the nonconformity happen? (e.g., Was it a lack of training, a flawed procedure, or a software bug?).
2. **Propose a Corrective Action Plan:** What steps will be taken to eliminate the root cause and prevent recurrence?
3. **Implement the Actions:** Execute the plan within an agreed timeframe.

#### 9.4.2 The Auditor's Responsibility
The auditor (or the certification body) plays a crucial role in verifying the fix:
1. **Review the Plan:** The auditor must review the proposed corrective action plan to ensure it actually addresses the root cause, not just the symptom. (e.g., If the symptom was a missing signature, the root cause might be an overly complex approval process. Just signing the paper doesn't fix the root cause).
2. **Verify Effectiveness:** Once the auditee claims the actions are implemented, the auditor must verify their effectiveness. This verification may be done through a review of documented evidence submitted by the auditee (a "desk review") or through a follow-up on-site visit, depending on the severity of the nonconformity.

Once the auditor is satisfied that the corrective action is effective and the nonconformity has been resolved, the nonconformity is officially "closed." For third-party audits, certification cannot be granted until all major nonconformities are closed.

### Knowledge Check and Reflection
**Question 1:** Why is it critical for the Lead Auditor to state during the closing meeting that the audit was based on a sampling process?
A) To excuse any mistakes the audit team might have made during the investigation.
B) To clarify that the absence of identified nonconformities does not guarantee that the AIMS is perfect and entirely free of issues.
C) To explain why the audit took less time than originally planned.
D) To justify asking the auditee for more documentation after the meeting has concluded.
*Answer: B*

**Question 2:** An auditor issues a nonconformity because an AI model was deployed without a privacy impact assessment. The auditee's corrective action plan states: "We will immediately conduct a privacy impact assessment for this specific model." As the Lead Auditor reviewing this plan, what is your assessment?
A) The plan is excellent and the nonconformity can be closed immediately.
B) The plan addresses the symptom (the missing assessment for one model) but fails to address the root cause (why the process allowed a model to be deployed without an assessment in the first place).
C) The plan is unacceptable because the auditor must conduct the privacy impact assessment.
D) The plan is fine, but they also need to fire the developer.
*Answer: B*

**Reflection Exercise:** Imagine you are presenting a major nonconformity to a CEO who becomes defensive and argues that the ISO standard is "too bureaucratic" for their fast-moving AI startup. How would you handle this situation during the closing meeting?
